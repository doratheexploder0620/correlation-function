Understanding Correlation Functions

1. Setting the Goal: Measuring Correlation

How can we tell if two fluctuating quantities, let's call them A and B, are related? Our initial goal is to find a way to quantify this relationship, or "correlation". Since these quantities often change unpredictably (maybe randomly, or over time and space), whatever measure we devise needs to reflect their overall statistical behavior. Therefore, we need to find a suitable statistical average that can act as our measure.

We'll use the notation $\langle \dots \rangle$ to represent taking an average. The mission is to find an averaged quantity that tells us if A and B are related in some way (i.e., if they are correlated), rather than being completely independent.

2. Hypothesis 1: The Average of Sums Approach

Let's test our first idea using a specific example. Imagine two particles, A and B, hopping around randomly on a grid. Let $\text{pos}_A$ and $\text{pos}_B$ represent their positions.

Uncorrelated Case

First, assume the particles hop completely independently of each other. We measure their positions many times and calculate the average of the sum, $\langle \text{pos}_A + \text{pos}_B \rangle$, and the sum of their averages, $\langle \text{pos}_A \rangle + \langle \text{pos}_B \rangle$.

Correlated Case

Now, let's introduce a correlation. Imagine the particles still hop randomly, but they are constrained to always remain a fixed distance apart. They are clearly correlated. Let's again calculate $\langle \text{pos}_A + \text{pos}_B \rangle$ and $\langle \text{pos}_A \rangle + \langle \text{pos}_B \rangle$ for this correlated motion.

Video 1b: Sums Approach (Correlated)

Description: Here, you'll see the correlated particles hopping while maintaining a fixed distance. The calculation still shows $\langle \text{pos}_A + \text{pos}_B \rangle = \langle \text{pos}_A \rangle + \langle \text{pos}_B \rangle$, just like in the uncorrelated case.

Conclusion for Sums

The videos confirm what the math tells us. Because averaging is linear ($\langle A + B \rangle = \langle A \rangle + \langle B \rangle$ always holds true), comparing the average of the sum to the sum of the averages gives the same result whether the particles are correlated or not. This measure is useless for detecting correlation.

3. Hypothesis 2: The Average of Products Approach

Since the sum approach failed, let's propose a different measure. What if we compare the average of the product of the positions, $\langle \text{pos}_A \times \text{pos}_B \rangle$, with the product of their average positions, $\langle \text{pos}_A \rangle \times \langle \text{pos}_B \rangle$? Let's test this using the same particle hopping example.

Uncorrelated Case Revisited

Consider the first scenario again, where particles A and B hop independently. We'll now calculate $\langle \text{pos}_A \times \text{pos}_B \rangle$ and $\langle \text{pos}_A \rangle \times \langle \text{pos}_B \rangle$.

Video 2a: Products Approach (Uncorrelated)

Description: This video demonstrates the product calculation for the uncorrelated particles. The simulation shows that $\langle \text{pos}_A \times \text{pos}_B \rangle$ is essentially equal to $\langle \text{pos}_A \rangle \times \langle \text{pos}_B \rangle$.

Correlated Case Revisited

Now, consider the second scenario where the particles hop randomly but maintain a fixed distance. We'll calculate $\langle \text{pos}_A \times \text{pos}_B \rangle$ and $\langle \text{pos}_A \rangle \times \langle \text{pos}_B \rangle$ for this correlated motion.

Video 2b: Products Approach (Correlated)

Description: Now watch the correlated particles. The simulation shows a clear difference: $\langle \text{pos}_A \times \text{pos}_B \rangle$ is not equal to $\langle \text{pos}_A \rangle \times \langle \text{pos}_B \rangle$.

Conclusion for Products

Success! The comparison involving products behaves differently in the uncorrelated versus correlated cases. The difference $\langle AB \rangle - \langle A \rangle \langle B \rangle$ (using general A and B notation now) deviates from zero when there is correlation. This establishes it as a potentially good statistical measure for detecting correlation.

4. Defining the Correlation Function

Since the product approach works so well, let's make it official. That useful difference we found has a name: the covariance:

$$ C_{AB} = \langle AB \rangle - \langle A \rangle \langle B \rangle $$

If the covariance isn't zero, it's a signal that A and B are correlated!

Often, we want to look at correlations more broadly, like how measurements relate across different times or locations. For things changing over time, $A(t)$ and $B(t)$, in a system whose basic properties don't change (it's "stationary"), we often use the time correlation function. It usually depends on the time gap $\tau = t_2 - t_1$:

$$ G(\tau) = \langle A(t) B(t+\tau) \rangle $$

This tells us, on average, how the value of $A$ right now is connected to the value of $B$ a time $\tau$ later.

We can do something similar for quantities that vary in space, like $A(\vec{r})$ and $B(\vec{r})$, defining spatial correlation functions based on the separation distance $\vec{r} = \vec{r}_2 - \vec{r}_1$.

5. Density Operator and Correlation Functions

Let's apply these ideas more formally to the density of particles in a system. Consider a system of $N$ particles, where the position of particle $\alpha$ is given by the dynamical variable $\vec{x}_\alpha$. The microscopic density at a point $\vec{x}$ is defined by the density operator (using the notation $n(\vec{x})$ from the reference):

$$ n(\vec{x}) = \sum_{\alpha=1}^{N} \delta(\vec{x} - \vec{x}_\alpha) \quad (*) $$

Its ensemble average $\langle n(\vec{x}) \rangle$ gives the average density at point $\vec{x}$. In homogeneous, isotropic fluids, $\langle n(\vec{x}) \rangle = n = N/V$ (the average number density), independent of $\vec{x}$.

The two-point density-density correlation function is:

$$ C_{nn}(\vec{x}_1, \vec{x}_2) = \langle n(\vec{x}_1) n(\vec{x}_2) \rangle = \left\langle \sum_{\alpha, \beta} \delta(\vec{x}_1 - \vec{x}_\alpha) \delta(\vec{x}_2 - \vec{x}_\beta) \right\rangle \quad (**) $$

Scattering experiments measure the structure function, $I(\vec{q})$, which is the Fourier transform of this correlation function:

$$ I(\vec{q}) = \int d^d x_1 \int d^d x_2 \, e^{-i \vec{q} \cdot (\vec{x}_1 - \vec{x}_2)} \langle n(\vec{x}_1) n(\vec{x}_2) \rangle \quad (***) $$

Let's show explicitly how this relates to the Fourier transform of the density operator itself. First, define the Fourier transform of $n(\vec{x})$:

$$ n(\vec{q}) = \int d^d x \, e^{-i \vec{q} \cdot \vec{x}} n(\vec{x}) = \sum_{\alpha=1}^{N} e^{-i \vec{q} \cdot \vec{x}_\alpha} \quad (****) $$

Now consider the ensemble average $\langle n(\vec{q}) n(-\vec{q}) \rangle$:

$$ \begin{align*} \langle n(\vec{q}) n(-\vec{q}) \rangle &= \left\langle \left( \int d^d x_1 \, e^{-i \vec{q} \cdot \vec{x}_1} n(\vec{x}_1) \right) \left( \int d^d x_2 \, e^{i \vec{q} \cdot \vec{x}_2} n(\vec{x}_2) \right) \right\rangle \\ &= \int d^d x_1 \int d^d x_2 \, e^{-i \vec{q} \cdot \vec{x}_1} e^{i \vec{q} \cdot \vec{x}_2} \langle n(\vec{x}_1) n(\vec{x}_2) \rangle \\ &= \int d^d x_1 \int d^d x_2 \, e^{-i \vec{q} \cdot (\vec{x}_1 - \vec{x}_2)} \langle n(\vec{x}_1) n(\vec{x}_2) \rangle \end{align*} $$

Comparing this result with the definition of $I(\vec{q})$ in Eq. (***), we see they are identical. Therefore:

$$ I(\vec{q}) = \langle n(\vec{q}) n(-\vec{q}) \rangle = \langle |n(\vec{q})|^2 \rangle \quad (*****) $$

Explanation for $n(\vec{q}) n(-\vec{q}) = |n(\vec{q})|^2$: This equality holds because the density $n(\vec{x})$ is a real-valued physical quantity. For any real function $f(x)$, its Fourier transform $f(q)$ has the property that $f(-q) = f(q)^*$, where $*$ denotes the complex conjugate. Let's verify this for $n(\vec{q})$:

$$ \begin{align*} n(\vec{q})^* &= \left( \int d^d x \, e^{-i \vec{q} \cdot \vec{x}} n(\vec{x}) \right)^* \\ &= \int d^d x \, (e^{-i \vec{q} \cdot \vec{x}})^* (n(\vec{x}))^* \quad \text{(Since integral and sum conjugates are conjugates of terms)} \\ &= \int d^d x \, e^{i \vec{q} \cdot \vec{x}} n(\vec{x}) \quad \text{(Since } n(\vec{x}) \text{ is real, } n(\vec{x})^* = n(\vec{x}) \text{)} \end{align*} $$

Now let's evaluate $n(-\vec{q})$:

$$ n(-\vec{q}) = \int d^d x \, e^{-i (-\vec{q}) \cdot \vec{x}} n(\vec{x}) = \int d^d x \, e^{i \vec{q} \cdot \vec{x}} n(\vec{x}) $$

Comparing the results, we see that $n(\vec{q})^* = n(-\vec{q})$. Therefore, $n(\vec{q}) n(-\vec{q}) = n(\vec{q}) n(\vec{q})^* = |n(\vec{q})|^2$.

This confirms that the structure function measured in scattering is precisely the ensemble average of the squared magnitude of the Fourier components of the density operator.

Explanation for Recoverability of $C_{nn}$: Can we get the full real-space correlation $C_{nn}(\vec{x}_1, \vec{x}_2)$ back from the measured $I(\vec{q})$? The relationship $I(\vec{q}) = \int d^d x_1 \int d^d x_2 \, e^{-i \vec{q} \cdot (\vec{x}_1 - \vec{x}_2)} C_{nn}(\vec{x}_1, \vec{x}_2)$ involves an integral over both $\vec{x}_1$ and $\vec{x}_2$, but the exponential factor only depends on their difference $\vec{r} = \vec{x}_1 - \vec{x}_2$. If $C_{nn}$ also depends *only* on the difference $\vec{r}$ (i.e., $C_{nn}(\vec{x}_1, \vec{x}_2) = C_{nn}(\vec{x}_1 - \vec{x}_2) = C_{nn}(\vec{r})$), as is the case in statistically homogeneous systems like fluids, then we can change integration variables to $\vec{r}$ and a center-of-mass coordinate $\vec{R}_{CM} = (\vec{x}_1 + \vec{x}_2)/2$. The integral over $\vec{R}_{CM}$ simply yields the volume $V$, leaving:

$$ I(\vec{q}) = V \int d^d r \, e^{-i \vec{q} \cdot \vec{r}} C_{nn}(\vec{r}) $$

In this situation, $I(\vec{q})$ is directly proportional to the standard Fourier transform of $C_{nn}(\vec{r})$. We can then uniquely determine $C_{nn}(\vec{r})$ by performing an inverse Fourier transform on the measured $I(\vec{q})$ (divided by $V$). However, if $C_{nn}(\vec{x}_1, \vec{x}_2)$ depends on $\vec{x}_1$ and $\vec{x}_2$ in a more complex way (e.g., in a crystal, where correlations depend on the absolute positions relative to the lattice sites, not just the separation), then $I(\vec{q})$ represents a projection or average of this complex information onto the separation vector dependence. Information about the dependence on the absolute positions is lost during the integration, and we cannot uniquely reconstruct the full $C_{nn}(\vec{x}_1, \vec{x}_2)$ just from $I(\vec{q})$.

6. The Ursell Function (Correlation of Fluctuations)

In analyzing density correlations, we often want to separate the trivial part arising from the product of average densities from the part that describes true correlations between fluctuations.

Let $n(\vec{x})$ be the density operator at position $\vec{x}$, and $\langle n(\vec{x}) \rangle$ be its ensemble average. The two-point density-density correlation function is $C_{nn}(\vec{x}_1, \vec{x}_2) = \langle n(\vec{x}_1) n(\vec{x}_2) \rangle$.

The Ursell function, denoted $S_{nn}(\vec{x}_1, \vec{x}_2)$, is defined by subtracting the product of the averages from the full correlation function:

$$ S_{nn}(\vec{x}_1, \vec{x}_2) = C_{nn}(\vec{x}_1, \vec{x}_2) - \langle n(\vec{x}_1) \rangle \langle n(\vec{x}_2) \rangle \quad (1) $$

Substituting the definition of $C_{nn}$:

$$ S_{nn}(\vec{x}_1, \vec{x}_2) = \langle n(\vec{x}_1) n(\vec{x}_2) \rangle - \langle n(\vec{x}_1) \rangle \langle n(\vec{x}_2) \rangle $$

This function measures the deviation of the full correlation from the simple product of averages, capturing the essence of how the densities at two points are statistically linked beyond their mean values.

Now, let's define the density fluctuation operator as the deviation of the local density from its average:

$$ \delta n(\vec{x}) = n(\vec{x}) - \langle n(\vec{x}) \rangle $$

We want to show that the Ursell function is equivalent to the correlation function of these fluctuations, $\langle \delta n(\vec{x}_1) \delta n(\vec{x}_2) \rangle$. Let's calculate this correlation explicitly:

$$ \begin{align*} \langle \delta n(\vec{x}_1) \delta n(\vec{x}_2) \rangle &= \langle [n(\vec{x}_1) - \langle n(\vec{x}_1) \rangle] [n(\vec{x}_2) - \langle n(\vec{x}_2) \rangle] \rangle \\ &= \langle n(\vec{x}_1)n(\vec{x}_2) - n(\vec{x}_1)\langle n(\vec{x}_2) \rangle - \langle n(\vec{x}_1) \rangle n(\vec{x}_2) + \langle n(\vec{x}_1) \rangle \langle n(\vec{x}_2) \rangle \rangle \\ &= \langle n(\vec{x}_1)n(\vec{x}_2) \rangle - \langle n(\vec{x}_1)\langle n(\vec{x}_2) \rangle \rangle - \langle \langle n(\vec{x}_1) \rangle n(\vec{x}_2) \rangle + \langle \langle n(\vec{x}_1) \rangle \langle n(\vec{x}_2) \rangle \rangle \\ &= \langle n(\vec{x}_1)n(\vec{x}_2) \rangle - \langle n(\vec{x}_1) \rangle \langle n(\vec{x}_2) \rangle - \langle n(\vec{x}_1) \rangle \langle n(\vec{x}_2) \rangle + \langle n(\vec{x}_1) \rangle \langle n(\vec{x}_2) \rangle \\ &= \langle n(\vec{x}_1)n(\vec{x}_2) \rangle - \langle n(\vec{x}_1) \rangle \langle n(\vec{x}_2) \rangle \end{align*} $$

Comparing this final result with the definition of $S_{nn}(\vec{x}_1, \vec{x}_2)$ in Eq. (1), we see they are identical.

Therefore, the Ursell function is precisely the correlation function of the density fluctuations:

$$ S_{nn}(\vec{x}_1, \vec{x}_2) = \langle \delta n(\vec{x}_1) \delta n(\vec{x}_2) \rangle \quad (2) $$

This function quantifies how fluctuations in density at one point are statistically related to fluctuations at another point. It decays to zero when the points are separated by more than the characteristic correlation length of the system.

Fourier Transform of the Ursell Function

We start with the Ursell function $S_{nn}(\vec{x}_1, \vec{x}_2) = \langle \delta n(\vec{x}_1) \delta n(\vec{x}_2) \rangle$. For a statistically homogeneous system, this depends only on the separation $\vec{r} = \vec{x}_1 - \vec{x}_2$, so we write $S_{nn}(\vec{r})$.

Let's consider the contribution of this function to the structure function $I(\vec{q})$. This contribution involves the double integral:

$$ \mathcal{I}_{Snn}(\vec{q}) = \int d^d x_1 \int d^d x_2 \, e^{-i \vec{q} \cdot (\vec{x}_1 - \vec{x}_2)} S_{nn}(\vec{x}_1 - \vec{x}_2) $$

This integral represents the unnormalized Fourier transform related to the fluctuation correlations.

To analyze its dependence on the system size (volume $V$), we perform a change of integration variables:

  • Separation vector: $\vec{r} = \vec{x}_1 - \vec{x}_2$
  • Center-of-mass coordinate: $\vec{R}_{CM} = (\vec{x}_1 + \vec{x}_2)/2$

The Jacobian for this transformation is 1, so the volume element $d^d x_1 d^d x_2$ becomes $d^d r d^d R_{CM}$. Substituting $\vec{x}_1 - \vec{x}_2 = \vec{r}$ and $S_{nn}(\vec{x}_1 - \vec{x}_2) = S_{nn}(\vec{r})$:

$$ \mathcal{I}_{Snn}(\vec{q}) = \int d^d R_{CM} \int d^d r \, e^{-i \vec{q} \cdot \vec{r}} S_{nn}(\vec{r}) $$

We can separate the integrals:

$$ \mathcal{I}_{Snn}(\vec{q}) = \left( \int d^d R_{CM} \right) \left( \int d^d r \, e^{-i \vec{q} \cdot \vec{r}} S_{nn}(\vec{r}) \right) $$

The first integral is over the center-of-mass coordinate, which spans the entire system volume:

$$ \int d^d R_{CM} = V $$

The second integral is the standard Fourier transform of the real-space Ursell function $S_{nn}(\vec{r})$. Let's denote this standard Fourier transform as $\tilde{S}_{nn}(\vec{q})$:

$$ \tilde{S}_{nn}(\vec{q}) = \int d^d r \, e^{-i \vec{q} \cdot \vec{r}} S_{nn}(\vec{r}) $$

Since $S_{nn}(\vec{r})$ describes correlations that decay over a finite length scale (independent of the total system size, for large systems), its integral $\tilde{S}_{nn}(\vec{q})$ is an intensive quantity, meaning it does not scale with the system volume $V$.

Substituting these back, we find the dependence of the unnormalized integral $\mathcal{I}_{Snn}(\vec{q})$ on the volume:

$$ \mathcal{I}_{Snn}(\vec{q}) = V \tilde{S}_{nn}(\vec{q}) \quad (3) $$

This result explicitly shows that the quantity $\mathcal{I}_{Snn}(\vec{q})$, which represents the contribution of fluctuation correlations to the structure function $I(\vec{q})$, is directly proportional to the system volume $V$. It is an extensive quantity.

In thermodynamics and statistical mechanics, it is usually preferable to work with intensive quantities that characterize the substance itself, independent of the amount of substance (or volume). Therefore, to obtain an intensive measure of the correlations in Fourier space, it is natural to divide the extensive quantity $\mathcal{I}_{Snn}(\vec{q})$ by the volume $V$. This leads to the definition used in Chaikin & Lubensky (Eq. 2.3.6):

$$ S_{nn}(\vec{q})_{\text{book}} = \frac{1}{V} \mathcal{I}_{Snn}(\vec{q}) = \frac{1}{V} \int d^d x_1 \int d^d x_2 \, e^{-i \vec{q} \cdot (\vec{x}_1 - \vec{x}_2)} S_{nn}(\vec{x}_1, \vec{x}_2) \quad (4) $$

As shown by Eq. (3), this definition yields:

$$ S_{nn}(\vec{q})_{\text{book}} = \frac{1}{V} (V \tilde{S}_{nn}(\vec{q})) = \tilde{S}_{nn}(\vec{q}) $$

So, the quantity $S_{nn}(\vec{q})$ defined with the $1/V$ normalization is indeed the standard, intensive Fourier transform of the real-space Ursell function $S_{nn}(\vec{r})$.

7. Structure Function: Average vs. Fluctuations

We previously established the relationship between the total structure function $I(\vec{q}) = \langle |n(\vec{q})|^2 \rangle$ and the intensive Fourier transform of the Ursell function, $S_{nn}(\vec{q}) = \int d^d r \, e^{-i \vec{q} \cdot \vec{r}} S_{nn}(\vec{r})$:

$$ I(\vec{q}) = |\langle n(\vec{q}) \rangle|^2 + V S_{nn}(\vec{q}) \quad (5) $$

where $\langle n(\vec{q}) \rangle = \int d^d x \, e^{-i \vec{q} \cdot \vec{x}} \langle n(\vec{x}) \rangle$ is the Fourier transform of the average density profile. This equation is fundamental as it separates the total scattering intensity $I(\vec{q})$ into two physically distinct contributions.

Scattering from the Average Structure: $|\langle n(\vec{q}) \rangle|^2$

The first term, $|\langle n(\vec{q}) \rangle|^2$, represents coherent scattering arising purely from the average spatial arrangement of particles in the system. To understand its dependence on the system size (volume $V$), consider the definition of $\langle n(\vec{q}) \rangle$:

$$ \langle n(\vec{q}) \rangle = \int_V d^d x \, e^{-i \vec{q} \cdot \vec{x}} \langle n(\vec{x}) \rangle $$

The integral is performed over the entire system volume $V$. If the average density profile $\langle n(\vec{x}) \rangle$ is non-zero over this volume (which is generally true, e.g., the constant density $n$ in a fluid or the periodic density in a crystal), the value of the integral will typically scale proportionally to the volume $V$, especially for wavevectors $\vec{q}$ that correspond to the average structure (like $\vec{q}=0$ for fluids, or the reciprocal lattice vectors $\vec{G}$ for crystals). Therefore, we can generally state that $\langle n(\vec{q}) \rangle \propto V$. Consequently, the scattering intensity arising from this average structure scales with the square of the volume:

$$ |\langle n(\vec{q}) \rangle|^2 \propto V^2 $$

This very strong $V^2$ dependence signifies that this scattering component arises from the coherent addition of waves scattered from the average structure across the entire macroscopic sample.

Case: Homogeneous Fluid: For a homogeneous fluid, the average density is constant, $\langle n(\vec{x}) \rangle = n$. Its Fourier transform is:

$$ \langle n(\vec{q}) \rangle = n \int_V d^d x \, e^{-i \vec{q} \cdot \vec{x}} $$

In the limit of an infinitely large volume, this integral becomes a Dirac delta function: $n (2\pi)^d \delta(\vec{q})$. For a large but finite volume $V$, the integral is sharply peaked around $\vec{q}=0$, and its value exactly at $\vec{q}=0$ is $\int_V d^d x \, n = n V$. This means $\langle n(\vec{q}) \rangle$ is effectively non-zero only for $\vec{q}=0$. As a result, the strong $V^2$ contribution to the total scattering $I(\vec{q})$ occurs only in the forward direction ($\vec{q}=0$). Using the formal relation for large $V$, we can approximate $|\langle n(\vec{q}) \rangle|^2 \approx |n(2\pi)^d \delta(\vec{q})|^2$. A more rigorous treatment involving the finite volume shows this term is proportional to $n^2 V (2\pi)^d \delta(\vec{q})$.

Scattering from Fluctuations: $V S_{nn}(\vec{q})$

The second term in Eq. (5), $V S_{nn}(\vec{q})$, represents the scattering contribution arising from the correlations in the density fluctuations $\delta n(\vec{x}) = n(\vec{x}) - \langle n(\vec{x}) \rangle$. As previously derived, $S_{nn}(\vec{q}) = \int d^d r \, e^{-i \vec{q} \cdot \vec{r}} S_{nn}(\vec{r})$ is the standard Fourier transform of the real-space Ursell function $S_{nn}(\vec{r})$. Because $S_{nn}(\vec{r})$ describes correlations that typically decay to zero over a finite microscopic length scale (the correlation length), independent of the total system size (for large systems), its Fourier transform $S_{nn}(\vec{q})$ is an intensive quantity. Consequently, the contribution $V S_{nn}(\vec{q})$ to the total scattering intensity $I(\vec{q})$ scales linearly with the system volume $V$. This $V$ dependence represents incoherent or diffuse scattering from local fluctuations throughout the sample volume.

Structure Factor per Volume $\mathcal{S}(\vec{q})$ for Fluids

It is often convenient to analyze the scattering intensity per unit volume, which we denote here as $\mathcal{S}(\vec{q}) = I(\vec{q})/V$. Let's apply this to a homogeneous fluid using Eq. (5). Substituting the expression for the average density term in a fluid (using d=3 dimensions for specificity):

$$ I(\vec{q}) \approx n^2 V (2\pi)^3 \delta(\vec{q}) + V S_{nn}(\vec{q}) $$

Now, dividing by the volume $V$ gives the intensity per unit volume:

$$ \mathcal{S}(\vec{q}) = \frac{I(\vec{q})}{V} \approx \frac{1}{V} [n^2 V (2\pi)^3 \delta(\vec{q}) + V S_{nn}(\vec{q})] $$ $$ \mathcal{S}(\vec{q}) \approx n^2 (2\pi)^3 \delta(\vec{q}) + S_{nn}(\vec{q}) $$

Rearranging gives the relationship:

$$ \mathcal{S}(\vec{q}) \approx S_{nn}(\vec{q}) + n^2(2\pi)^3\delta(\vec{q}) \quad (6) $$

This equation provides a clear interpretation for scattering from fluids:

  • At $\vec{q}=0$: The scattering intensity per unit volume $\mathcal{S}(\vec{q})$ diverges due to the delta function, reflecting the coherent forward scattering from the average density $n$.
  • For $\vec{q} \neq 0$: The delta function term is zero. Therefore, $\mathcal{S}(\vec{q}) = S_{nn}(\vec{q})$. The scattering intensity per unit volume away from the forward direction is identical to the intensive Fourier transform of the Ursell function. It directly measures the correlations in density fluctuations. $S_{nn}(\vec{q}=0)$ itself remains finite and is related to macroscopic thermodynamic properties like the isothermal compressibility.

Interpreting Scattering in Other Systems

The general separation $I(\vec{q}) = |\langle n(\vec{q}) \rangle|^2 + V S_{nn}(\vec{q})$ remains valid:

Ideal Gas: In an ideal gas, particles are completely uncorrelated. This means $S_{nn}(\vec{r})=0$ for $\vec{r} \neq 0$. The only contribution is the self-correlation term, which leads to $S_{nn}(\vec{q}) = n$ (constant). The average density is $\langle n(\vec{x}) \rangle = n$. So, $I(\vec{q}) = |n(2\pi)^d \delta(\vec{q})|^2 + V n$. For $\vec{q} \neq 0$, $I(\vec{q}) = V n = N$. This corresponds to incoherent scattering from $N$ independent particles.

Periodic Solids: In a perfect crystal, the average density $\langle n(\vec{x}) \rangle$ is a periodic function with the symmetry of the lattice. Its Fourier transform $\langle n(\vec{q}) \rangle$ is non-zero only at the specific wavevectors corresponding to the reciprocal lattice vectors, $\vec{q} = \vec{G}$. At these vectors, the term $|\langle n(\vec{q}) \rangle|^2$ is proportional to $V^2$ and gives rise to the sharp, intense Bragg peaks characteristic of crystalline order. Between these Bragg peaks ($\vec{q} \neq \vec{G}$), the average structure term $|\langle n(\vec{q}) \rangle|^2$ is zero. The scattering intensity in these regions is given entirely by the fluctuation term $I(\vec{q}) = V S_{nn}(\vec{q})$. This $S_{nn}(\vec{q})$ now represents correlations in the fluctuations of atoms around their average lattice positions (due to thermal vibrations, i.e., phonons) or correlations due to defects. This contribution results in weaker, continuous diffuse scattering observed between the sharp Bragg peaks.

8. The Pair Distribution Function $g(\vec{x})$

Introduction: What is $g(\vec{x})$?

Imagine looking at a system of particles (like a liquid or gas). If you pick one specific particle and conceptually place it at the origin of your coordinate system, the pair distribution function, $g(\vec{x})$, tells you how likely you are to find another particle at a position $\vec{x}$ relative to the one at the origin.

More precisely, if the average number density of particles throughout the system is $n$, then $n g(\vec{x})$ represents the average density you would observe at point $\vec{x}$ knowing that there is a particle fixed at the origin.

  • If $g(\vec{x}) = 1$, the density at $\vec{x}$ is just the average density $n$. This means the particle at the origin has no influence on the density at $\vec{x}$, which happens for large distances or in a completely random system (ideal gas).
  • If $g(\vec{x}) > 1$, particles are more likely to be found at separation $\vec{x}$ than purely by chance (e.g., forming shells of nearest neighbors).
  • If $g(\vec{x}) < 1$, particles are less likely to be found at separation $\vec{x}$ (e.g., $g(\vec{x}) \approx 0$ for small $|\vec{x}|$ because particles cannot overlap).

Calculating $g(\vec{x})$: Eq. 2.3.12 Explained

One way to formalize the calculation of $g(\vec{x})$ in a homogeneous system is given by the expression:

$$ g(\vec{x}) = \frac{1}{n} \left\langle \sum_{\alpha' \neq 0} \delta(\vec{x} - (\vec{x}_{\alpha'} - \vec{x}_0)) \right\rangle \quad (7) $$

Let's break this down:

  • $\vec{x}_0$: We conceptually fix one particle (labeled 0) at the origin.
  • $\vec{x}_{\alpha'}$: Represents the position of any other particle ($\alpha' \neq 0$).
  • $\vec{x}_{\alpha'} - \vec{x}_0$: This is the vector pointing from the origin particle (0) to particle $\alpha'$.
  • $\delta(\vec{x} - (\vec{x}_{\alpha'} - \vec{x}_0))$: This delta function picks out only those particles $\alpha'$ whose separation from particle 0 is exactly $\vec{x}$.
  • $\sum_{\alpha' \neq 0} \dots$: We sum these delta functions over all particles other than the one at the origin. This sum gives the instantaneous density of other particles at separation $\vec{x}$ from particle 0 in a specific configuration.
  • $\langle \dots \rangle$: We take the ensemble average over all possible configurations (all allowed positions of all particles) consistent with the system's thermodynamics. This gives the average density of other particles at separation $\vec{x}$ from particle 0.
  • $\frac{1}{n}$: We divide by the average overall density $n$ to get the relative density, which is the definition of $g(\vec{x})$.

The crucial assumption for this specific form is homogeneity. Because the system is homogeneous, the average environment around any particle is the same. Therefore, it doesn't matter which specific particle we choose to label as '0' and place at the origin; the resulting average density profile of neighbors relative to it will be identical. This justifies calculating the average based on fixing just one particle.

Intuitive Method for Determining $g(\vec{x})$

Equation (7) leads to a direct, practical way to estimate $g(\vec{x})$ from simulations or even conceptually:

  1. Take a snapshot (a configuration) of all particle positions.
  2. Pick one particle (particle A) and consider it to be at the origin.
  3. Look at a small volume element $d^d x$ located at position $\vec{x}$ relative to particle A.
  4. Count how many other particles fall within this volume element.
  5. Repeat steps 2-4, choosing different particles (B, C, ...) as the origin particle.
  6. Average the count obtained in step 4 over all the chosen origin particles. Let this average count be $\langle \text{Count}(\vec{x}, d^d x) \rangle$.
  7. The pair distribution function is then obtained by normalizing this average count by the number of particles you'd expect in that volume purely by chance, which is $n d^d x$:
$$ g(\vec{x}) = \frac{\langle \text{Count}(\vec{x}, d^d x) \rangle}{n d^d x} $$

Relation to Scattering: Eq. 2.3.13 Explained

A key result connects the pair distribution function $g(\vec{x})$ to the experimentally measurable scattering. Often, this is expressed via the static structure factor $S(\vec{q})$, typically defined as the total scattering intensity $I(\vec{q})$ divided by the number of particles $N$ (i.e., $S(\vec{q}) = I(\vec{q})/N$). For wavevectors $\vec{q} \neq 0$, this structure factor is related to $g(\vec{x})$ by:

$$ S(\vec{q}) = 1 + n \int d^d x \, e^{-i \vec{q} \cdot \vec{x}} (g(\vec{x}) - 1) \quad (\text{for } \vec{q} \neq 0) \quad (8) $$

Why is this important?

  • It shows that the way particles scatter waves (like X-rays or neutrons) at different angles (related to $\vec{q}$) depends directly on how particles are arranged relative to each other in real space (described by $g(\vec{x})$).
  • The '1' corresponds to the scattering from individual particles if they were uncorrelated (the "self" scattering part).
  • The integral term involves the Fourier transform of the total correlation function $h(\vec{x}) = g(\vec{x}) - 1$. It represents the contribution from the interference of waves scattered by different, correlated particles.
  • This equation allows us to work backward: by measuring $S(\vec{q})$ experimentally (for $\vec{q} \neq 0$), we can perform an inverse Fourier transform to calculate $h(\vec{x})$ and thus $g(\vec{x})$ (or $g(r)$ for isotropic systems), revealing the microscopic structure of the material.
